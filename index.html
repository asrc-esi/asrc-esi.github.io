About Us

Our mission is to create an objective, practical, and long-term process for evaluating the degree to which corporate facility operations support or compromise environmental sustainability, and thus their viability as environmentally responsible investments.

Ecosystem services, such as access to clean water, are essential for human life and economic development.
Interconnected global environmental challenges require more than man-made (engineered) solutions, such as dams.
Green infrastructure investments, such as bioswails, can provide benefits not just to humans, but also to the environment.
keyboard_arrow_left keyboard_arrow_right

Environmental and ecosystem services are essential for human life and are critical for economic development. Growing demands for environmental services will require new approaches for sustainable use, not just through use of and investment in technology but also through new and integrated ways of assessment.

Building on our legacy of environmental research, we have developed a novel approach that fuses corporate information with environmental assessments at the global scale to understand the local impacts of corporations on environmental systems/nature, particularly water.

We strive to:

  • Help businesses and investors to identify and measure their environmental-related risks and impacts
  • Provide a robust systematic framework with traceable indicators of environmental system impacts
  • Measure the impact of company-specific activities on the security of ecosystem services and ecosystem sustainability
  • Identify business and investment opportunities to increase ecosystem services for people and businesses

We provide new ways of assessing businesses by taking into consideration green infrastructure benefits not just to humans, but also to the environment. The interconnected nature of global environmental challenges makes it insufficient to only rely on man-made (engineered) solutions.

We are located in the Environmental Sciences Initiative of the CUNY Advanced Science Research Center, in New York City.

Selected Environmental Datasets from the CUNY Advanced Science Research Center

  • Population Supported by Renewable Water Resources
  • Investments Benefit Factor
  • Spatial Distribution of Dam Density in River Basins
  • Relative Water Stress Index
  • Exposure of Population to Impaired Water Resource

Population supported by ecosystem services (freshwater provisioning) gives an estimate of the number of people who are served by upstream renewable freshwater services. This metric provides a more direct estimate of water source areas and maps the quantity of these services serving downstream, dependent populations.

Source: Green et al. (2015): Freshwater ecosystem services supporting humans: Pivoting from water crisis to water solutions. Global Environmental Change 34:108-118.

A metric for the degree to which threats to human water security have been improved through technological and engineering approaches. This metric identifies regions that would benefit from technological improvements to water resources and can be updated statistically. It is a weighted index of key water resource investments, such as in reservoirs and water treatment facilities.

Source: Vörösmarty et al. (2010): Global threats to human water security and river biodiversity. Nature 467: 555-561.

A probabilistic pixel level estimation of the density of dams globally. A simple aggregate measure of the density of investments in water storage facilities, developed currently from statistical analysis of dam and reservoir size distributions in the United States.

Source: Vörösmarty et al. (2010): Global threats to human water security and river biodiversity. Nature 467: 555-561.

This index provides a measure of the water demand pressures from the domestic, industrial, and agricultural sectors relative to local upstream water supplies. This can be integrated with population data. When integrated with population data, the index easily identifies highly populated areas under water stress. Satellite inputs driving water supply models can be updated frequently and are globally consistent.

Source: Vörösmarty et al. (2000). Global water resources: Vulnerability from climate change and population growth. Science 289: 284-288.

A measure of population whose water resource systems are under potential human water security incident threats. Provides a more direct estimate of the condition of water sources than previously available that serve downstream, dependent populations. Incorporates all major classes of anthropogenic drivers of stress and enables an assessment of their aggregate impact.

Source: Vörösmarty et al. (2010): Global threats to human water security and river biodiversity. Nature 467: 555-561.

Team

Charles J. Vörösmarty, PhD

charles.vorosmarty@asrc.cuny.edu

Charles Vörösmarty, PhD is the Founding Director of the Environmental Sciences Initiative at the CUNY Advanced Science Research Center. His research focuses on the development of computer models and geospatial data sets used in synthesis studies of the interactions among the water cycle, climate, biogeochemistry and anthropogenic activities. His studies are built around local, regional and continental to global-scale modeling of water balance, discharge, constituent fluxes in river systems and the analysis of the impacts of large-scale water engineering on the terrestrial water cycle. His work on human-water interactions includes earth system modeling of the Northeastern United States, development and analysis of databases depicting reservoir construction worldwide and how they generate downstream coastal zone risks, and global threats to human water security and aquatic biodiversity.

He routinely provides scientific guidance to a variety of U.S. and international water consortia. He is a founding member and current co-Chair of the Global Water System Project that represents the input of several hundred international scientists under the International Council for Science’s Global Environmental Change Programs. He is spearheading efforts to develop global-scale indicators of water stress and is working with chief United Nations delegates who are negotiating the Rio+20 Sustainable Development Goals. He has served on a broad array of national panels, including the U.S. Arctic Research Commission (appointed by Presidents Bush and Obama), the NASA Earth Science Subcommittee, the National Research Council Committee on Hydrologic Science as Chair, a member of the NRC Review Committee on the U.S. Global Change Research Program, the National Science Foundation’s Arctic System Science Program Committee, and the Arctic HYDRA International Polar Year Planning Team. He also was a consultant to the 24-agency United Nations World Water Assessment Programme and represented the International Council of Scientific Unions at the UN Commission on Sustainable Development meetings.

Anthony D. Cak, PhD

anthony.cak@asrc.cuny.edu

Anthony D. Cak, PhD is the Associate Director of the Environmental Sciences Initiative at the CUNY Advanced Science Research Center. He received his PhD in 2011 from Indiana University-Bloomington. His dissertation focused on the impacts of deforestation and urban development on the water chemistry of small streams in the Brazilian Amazon, in and near the city of Altamira in the state of Pará. His research interests include ecosystem ecology, stream and river ecology, geospatial technology, data visualizations, and science policy and communication. He received his BS and MS in 2002 and 2005, respectively, from the University of Notre Dame.

Prior to joining the ASRC, Dr. Cak was a Program Analyst in the Integrative Organismal Systems Division of the Biological Sciences Directorate of the National Science Foundation, and before that, a Program Specialist in the Emerging Frontiers Division of the Biological Sciences Directorate of the National Science Foundation.

Vanesa Rodríguez Osuna, PhD

vanesa.rodriguez.osuna@asrc.cuny.edu

Vanesa Rodríguez Osuna, PhD is a Senior Research Associate at the Environmental Sciences Initiative. She serves as a project manager to measure the social/ecological impacts of global equities associated with water. She is leading the development of robust methodology and research approaches to quantify corporate-specific impacts on global water resources, as well as overseeing project outcomes and outreach efforts with the research sponsor (UBS Sustainable Equities/Asset Management) and collaborating partners (Harvard University).

Trained as an environmental engineer, Dr. Rodríguez Osuna received a M.Sc. in Natural Resource Management and Technologies in the Tropics from the Cologne University of Applied Sciences and a PhD in Geography from the University of Leipzig, with specializations in Project Management, Foreign Trade Management and Quality & Environmental Management Systems. She is a lead author of the Intergovernmental Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services (IPBES)’s regional assessment of biodiversity and ecosystem services for the Americas.

Her past work relates to the sustainable use of biodiversity, integrated land and water management, climate change, environmental and economic assessment of ecosystem services, environmental policy, environmental impact assessment, alternative market development, and bridging the science-policy interface related to biodiversity and ecosystem services. Within these fields, she has worked for the Deutsche Gesellschaft für Internationale Zusammenarbeit (GIZ), Conservation Strategy Fund, RARE, Intergovernmental Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services (IPBES) Secretariat/UNEP, and SEQUA gGmbH.

Pamela Green, ME

pgreen.ccny@gmail.com

Pamela Green, ME is a Senior Research Associate at the Environmental Sciences Initiative. She earned an M.E. in Environmental Engineering from the University of Florida in 1992 with directed study in surface water hydrology and water resource analysis. Ms. Green is a surface-water hydrologist and geospatial analyst with more than 15 years of experience with GIS and water-resource analysis.

Her current research focuses on continental to global scale modeling of surface water systems, including evaluating impacts of human perturbations across terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems.

She is part of projects include investigating the impact of climate variability and human water pressure on the global hydrologic cycle, freshwater resources and freshwater biodiversity. Ms. Green has participated in the design and development of web-based tools to provide water managers and policy makers with information on freshwater resources and quantitative indicators for water resources condition and stress at the global, regional, river basin and country scales.
